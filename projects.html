Where's Schueller?

This interactive bar chart shows the overall time that Professor Schueller spent in various states (excluding Washington, as his home state), according to his phone's location data. This project involved data reading, sorting, and analyzing latitutde and longitude, and using the Haversine formula and geomapping to convert the data points into useable data that could be associated with different states. This project was completed as part of a Data Science course.

Temple Traders

Contributors: Stella Dong, LJ Friedman, Carolyn Jones, Ariel Moncrief, and Anders Taylor

Our final group project for the Spring 2024 semester in Game Development was this 3D first person shooter game we called Temple Traders. We went through many design iterations for this project, as we had many ideas but wanted to ensure we had a cohesive game design that could also be reasonably completed within a month. We initially wanted to focus on some kind of arena game, and had ideas like environmental-based ways to defeat enemies, as well as a system similar to Little Alchemy that would allow you to combine aspects of the arena to create special abilities. However, our professor advised us against trying to create crafting systems, as those typically end up taking longer than we would have time for. Instead, we focused on creating an upgrade system to buy different guns, speed upgrades, and health.

My major contribution was creating the enemies. Initially I had ideas for 8 different classes of enemies, but was having difficulties finding different animated meshes for the project (we did not know any art students familiar enough with Blender or a similar 3D software that could create models for us). In the end, only 3 free meshes were available and distinct enough for the project. The projectiles generated were not attached to the animations of the creatures, so I had to ensure the projectiles being thrown looked as if they were coming from the hand of the creature.

Die...No!

Contributors: Stella Dong, LJ Friedman, Carolyn Jones, Ariel Moncrief, and Anders Taylor

This group project was completed under the supervision of Michael Tran, who was our professor at DIS in Copenhagen, Denmark while we studied abroad in the Spring of 2024. The purpose of this project was to explore 2D game design and all its elements, including “juice” (extra effects that immerse the reader), animation, movements and player controllers, and collisions and physics interactions. My primary contribution to this project was with the animation and handling animation triggers and events for all animated sprites.

Our initial plan for this game was to create a 2d platform game with some element of corruption, which we decided would best be represented by the transformation of the dinosaurs from gross-looking blobs to their happy colorful selves. Initially, we wanted the game to be focused on finding more complicated things to help “unlock” the dinosaurs and allow them to be free from their corrupted prison, but decided that didn’t give the player much motivation for freeing the dinosaurs. Instead, we decided to have the unlocked dinosaurs assist the player by standing on buttons and acting as platforms that can be jumped on. Had we had more time with this game, we would have added more complicated levels, as well as obstacles (such as spikes), and changed the visuals of the environment across each level. We also would have provided a more expansive cutscene at the beginning to explain how the dinos became corrupted and why you’re being tasked with finding them.

Composition Panel

This project is a music composition creator that was created over a semester in a Software Design class. Each week, we would have new requirements for the composition panel that required us to rethink our initial structure’s design. The purpose of the class was to teach us about code structure, design, and ways to minimize technical debt. The final product was created as a team featuring myself, Andrew Kuhlken, Henry Young, and Diego Quispe Vilcahuaman.

The project’s goal was to create an accessible way to create music for people without any formal music training.

The following is a list of the program’s main features:

  • Add and delete notes
  • Change selected instruments
  • Play and stop the composition
  • Select a single note, multiple notes, and all notes in the panel
  • Group and ungroup notes (includes nested grouping)
  • Copy/paste
  • Edit length/duration of selected notes
  • Move selected notes
  • Save, create, and load existing compositions
  • Customizable note snapping, allowing notes to be aligned perfectly with each other

Casti Calendar

This was one of my first big programming projects. I and two other students learned Swift in our high school’s Advanced Topics Computer Science class. Our school’s schedule was intentionally designed to be random, which meant that every day would have different classes in a different order. Our goal was to use the .csv file that the school created annually of the student schedule to produce a working customizable calendar based on individual classes and preferences. My group consisted of myself, Michelle Xu, and Linnea Leaver. The following list showcases some of the main features of the project.

  • Themed home page, based on the school’s colors for each grade level
  • Instructions page, that explains the features available
  • Weekly and daily schedule views, color-coded by class
  • Settings page, to allow the student to select their grade level, and customize the names and colors of each of their classes

Deriving National Happiness

By LJ Friedman and Rena Takahashi

Introduction

We used multiple data sets for our analysis. Our main focus was on the List of Happiest Countries Rankings in 2024 data (World Population Review), and we wanted to see how the following other data sets interacted with the happiest country rankings: Cost of Living Rankings by Country in 2024 (World Population Review); Health Expenditure per Capita by Country in 2021 , Average Annual Precipitation by Country in 2021 , and the Percentage of Land Dedicated for Agriculture in 2021 (World Bank Group). Using these data, we explored the question: What is the relationship between a country’s economic and natural resources, and the country’s overall happiness level?

To answer this question, we looked at four resources of each country: cost of living, healthcare expenditure per capita, annual precipitation, and percentage of land occupied for agriculture. Agriculture and precipitation were used for a few reasons. While they are not directly tied to economics, they are indicators of whether or not the country’s land is used to stimulate the economy. Although everyone needs to eat, not everywhere can grow food. Access to agricultural land and water sources (such as rain) thus become crucial for supporting a population. But does the ability to generate a surplus of food impact how happy a country is? Or is the field of agriculture working against happiness?

Our approach to the data was to first examine the data on choropleth maps, to see if there were any interesting or notable geographical similarities or differences to explore. Then, we examined the relationships between happiness and our other variables using scatter and box plots, to determine if there was any relationship between them. We used four quartiles of happiness for the box plots, in a style reminiscent of Hans Rosling’s four levels of wealth.

Data Preparation

We initially planned to work with the Youth Risk Behavior Surveillance System, but we were unable to find a simple way to convert the data into a comma-separated value (CSV) format. Because of this, we decided to pivot into using smaller more specialized datasets, and simply pull from multiple sources and sets to use for our data analysis.

When preparing the data, we first had to import the .csv files and load them in as dataframes. From there, we selected the columns that we were interested in, and combined them all from every data set into one dataframe that contained all of the information we were interested in. Then, we pulled from a .json file on Github to ensure we could create proper maps of the countries to visualize our data. Notably, some of the country names were inconsistent across data sets, and this was impacting our ability to merge the data together. To solve this, I created a list of mismatched country names, and converted the data set to use the .json’s country names, to ensure that we could visualize them.

There were also some issues where the datasets themselves had small issues, inclusions, or exclusions that were superfluous. Some data sets contained sovereign city-states (Monaco), which we opted to exclude as they were not in the .json mapping data. There were also several countries that did not have recent data, but had older data, and in these cases we opted to use the most recent data possible (for instance, Syria was missing health expenditure data past 2012). This may have a small impact on our data analysis, although these are not a large number of countries.
